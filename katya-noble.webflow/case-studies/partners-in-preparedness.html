Emergency Resource Cards for Senior Citizens

Overview

Emergency preparedness is a universal need. The majority of the human population is impacted by natural disasters or medial emergencies at some point in their lives.

Historically, seniors are one of the most vulnerable populations. For low-income seniors, basic needs such as housing, food, and medical care are not met.

Client: Serving Seniors in Downtown San Diego

Teammates: Nayeli Ruiz, Grace Chen, Kayleigh Mitchell, Andrea Suddartha

Story

We teamed up with Serving Seniors, a non-profit providing 5,000 seniors annually with the housing, nutritional, educational, health, and social support that they need. Serving Seniors serves seniors under the federal poverty level who struggle to meet their daily basic needs.

Our final prototype was a set of emergency rescue cards that seniors can carry in their bus pass holder or wallet. This:
‍

  • allows emergency responders to help them when they’re in need
  • gives seniors and responders the information of who to call in case of an emergency
  • gives seniors and responders the information of who to call in case of an emergency

Challenge

Helping seniors in San Diego living in poverty be prepared for emergencies

Outcome

Physical Emergency Resource Cards seniors can carry, containing resources for the seniors and first-responders

Scope

Low-income seniors in Downtown San Diego seeking resources from Serving Seniors+  Serving Seniors staff

Initial Insights

Seniors
We volunteered, surveyed, and interviewed seniors.

‍
  • Seniors tend to stick to themselves or to people they know
    ‍
  • Low-income seniors prioritize living day-to-day rather than making long-term plans
    ‍
  • Many considered emergency preparedness to be very important, including having identification, but did not prioritize it in their lives
    ‍
First Responders
We interviewed the San Diego Fire Dept, local paramedics, and emergency management services.
‍
‍
  • Having a plan and a community is an important aspect of emergency preparedness
    ‍
  • Having identification at all times is   extremely important

    ‍
Serving Seniors Staff
We interviewed the administration, social workers, nurses, and volunteers of Serving Seniors.

‍
  • Seniors often lose, misplace, or get their identification stolen
    ‍
  • Identification is important to low-income seniors in order to get access to many vital services
    ‍
  • Seniors should be part of a community
    ‍
  • Emergency kits are helpful, but aren’t    designed to solve senior-specific issues
    ‍
  • Mobility is restricted by the amount of    things they carry, their age, and location
How can we support and encourage seniors to think about the importance of identification and community in case of an emergency?

Our first few ideas presented some problems. Certain forms of our ideas were not impactful or requiring too much effort for the seniors. We wanted our design to be daring, feasible, and impactful.

Prototypes

Emergency Buddies
Initial Prototype
Seniors would partner up with another senior as their emergency buddy, make bead key chains for each other, and help each other fill out their cards.
Testing:

At the civic engagement meeting at Serving Seniors, 15 seniors tested out our program.

Feedback:
  • Medication card should specify dosage
    ‍
  • Medical conditions card should specify if they have visual    and/or hearing impairments + a section for mental health
    ‍
  • Allergies card should also include indication for   unwanted medication
Takeaways:
  • They enjoyed having physical cards
    ‍
  • They asked to keep the Resources card
    ‍
  • They showed a lack of interest in the buddy activity
Emergency Resource Cards
Final Prototype
Our final prototype was tailored toward the issues relevant to our target demographic. The cards aimed to be easily translatable, scalable, non-intrusive, and educational.

Implementation

We distributed the Emergency Rescue Cards at the Gary and Mary West Wellness Center in Serving Seniors. To cater to seniors’ daily routine, we set up a table in the  cafeteria during multiple lunch sessions.
Day 1:
  • Set up in cafeteria while seniors waited to get lunch
    ‍
  • Had cookies, cards, and lanyards with clear badge holder
    ‍
  • Seniors lined up before receiving their lunch to get a set of cards, lanyard and badge holder, and a cookie
Issues:
  • Seniors were incentivized by the cookies and didn’t fill out the cards
    ‍
  • Confusion due to the lack of descriptions on the cards
    ‍
  • Language barriers (large Mandarin and Spanish    population)
    ‍
  • Seniors struggled filling out the medication section
Day 2:
  • Set up in the cafeteria with rescue cards, laminating paper, and cookies
    ‍
  • Seniors came up to us out of their own interest
    ‍
  • Filled out cards in front of us or at their table
Changes:
  • Made announcements in English and Mandarin
    ‍
  • Described what each card’s purpose was
    ‍
  • Gave cookies AFTER they filled out the cards
    ‍
  • Helped them fill out the cards and helped laminate
Implementation 2: Seniors filling out their Emergency Resource Cards with the help of the Partners in Preparedness team.

Wrap-Up

Throughout the duration of this project, we tested our assumptions and learned more about senior needs. For example, we learned that though some seniors reported problems of isolation, many within Serving Seniors created a community among themselves.

For future iterations, the rescue cards should be distributed at different locations. Since many seniors had questions about medication, a possible method to distribute the cards would be through the nurses at Serving Seniors. Nurses would be able to provide one-on-one help with the medical cards. Social workers at Serving Seniors would also be another channel for distribution since they have meetings with seniors. Pairing the rescue cards with a program to strengthen the community would be ideal for future interactions.
